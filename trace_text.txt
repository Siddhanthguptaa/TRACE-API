TRACE: Graph-Aware Anomaly Detection for Trust Routing in
Adversarial Decentralized Marketplaces
Anonymous Author(s)
Abstract
Decentralized agent marketplaces route paid work to pseudony-
mous providers. Any trust mechanism balances three pressures:
bound fraud exposure, stay open to newcomers, and survive co-
ordinated graph manipulation. We compare four routing policies
(reputation-only, price-only, EigenTrust, and Trust Routing with Ad-
versarial Coordination Evidence (TRACE), which combines uncertainty-
aware evidence weighting with structural coordination detection
in place of trust propagation) across four attack regimes at N=1,000
agents with 30% adversaries, ten seeds per cell. Two results drive
the story. Trust propagation amplifies coordinated attacks: Eigen-
Trust routes 81.7% of jobs to malicious agents under Sybil attack,
while reputation-only contains it to 29.0%. Exploration is also costly
under score-based adversaries. A component ablation shows that
disabling Thompson sampling in TRACE reduces mean fraud from
10.7, 46.5, 19.0, and 83.7 sats to 4.7, 6.4, 7.1, and 31.7 sats on strategic-
default, collusion-ring, sybil-cluster, and game-theoretic-adversary.
The greedy variant has the lowest fraud on the first three regimes;
EigenTrust attains lower game-theoretic fraud (4.4) but at 41.2%
malicious routing rate. A single-seed run at N=5,000 confirms Eigen-
Trust’s pathology persists at scale, and the propagating versus non-
propagating ordering replicates on real-world signed-trust graphs
(Bitcoin Over-The-Counter, Bitcoin Alpha). The findings caution
against trust propagation in adversarial settings and motivate risk-
conditioned exploration as a principled extension.

1 Introduction
When an autonomous software agent pays another agent five
satoshis to summarize a document, the transaction settles in un-
der a second over the Lightning Network. Neither party knows
the other’s real identity. There is no contract, no escrow, no legal
recourse if the provider takes the payment and returns garbage.
The routing algorithm-the mechanism that decided which provider

As agent-to-agent marketplaces grow from dozens to thousands
of pseudonymous providers, routing stops being a matching prob-
lem and becomes an adversarial one. Coordinated attackers can
manufacture trust signals, attract routing, and drain irreversible
micropayments before anyone notices.
For two decades, trust propagation was the default remedy. Kam-
var, Schlosser, and Garcia-Molina [1] built EigenTrust on a simple
premise: model the P2P rating graph as a Markov chain and take
each node’s global trust as the stationary distribution. The wa-
ger is transitivity - Alice trusts Bob, Bob trusts Carol, so Alice
assigns partial credit to Carol. File sharing had one presiding failure
mode (free-riding), and EigenTrust addressed it. Resnick et al. [2]
had shown earlier that pseudonyms and feedback can together
sustain cooperation; Dellarocas [3] catalogued the failure modes -
ballot-stuffing, bad-mouthing, and whitewashing by re-registering.
Manipulation is inexpensive when the settlement exceeds the cost
of a new identity.
None of these developments truly prepared the field for the rise
of agent marketplaces. One attacker can make dozens of identities
(the Sybil attack, per Douceur [4]), earn trust edges on small, low-
value jobs, and then switch to stealing real payments. Colluders
can quietly inflate each other’s ratings for weeks before exploiting
the inflated scores. Adaptive adversaries check their own standing
and only cheat when they can tank the reputation hit without
losing routing share. File-sharing lost you a download; here you
lose satoshis that you cannot recover.
Despite two decades of research on distributed trust, no empiri-
cal evaluation has stressed routing mechanisms under combined
coordinated attacks at population scale with real payment exposure.
EigenTrust [1] was validated against non-coordinating free-riders
in file-sharing topologies where honest nodes held a structural
majority. SybilGuard and SybilLimit assumed sparse cuts between
honest and Sybil regions-an assumption that collapses when adver-
saries can inject edges by completing cheap transactions. Whether
exploration-exploitation strategies from the multi-armed bandit
literature help or hurt under adversarial load has remained an open
question, because standard bandit formulations treat arm quality
as fixed or stochastically varying, not as strategically adversarial
with the ability to manufacture trust edges and coordinate across
identities.
We address this gap through controlled experiments on TRACE-
a trust-routing framework we built and deployed as a Lightning-
powered agent marketplace called SatsRouter. We compare four
routing policies-reputation-only, price-only, EigenTrust [1], and
TRACE-across four attack types at N=1,000 agents with 30% ad-
versaries, running ten independent seeds per experimental cell.
Two findings anchor the paper. Trust propagation makes things
worse: EigenTrust routed 81.7% of all jobs to malicious agents under
Sybil attack, while a simple reputation-weighted policy without any

The marketplace substrate is not hypothetical. SatsRouter is a de-
ployed Lightning-powered agent marketplace where artificial intel-
ligence agents hire specialist agents and pay per request in satoshis
(5–20 sats per job). The system routes real micropayments over
the Lightning Network, with L402 paywalls gating each agent end-
point. The experimental evaluation uses SatsRouter’s architecture-
its provider registry, job routing, reputation tracking, and payment
settlement-as the simulation substrate. The four routing policies are
implemented as configurable modules within this deployed system.
The simulation differs from production in two respects: (a) provider
Behaviour is controlled by attack scripts rather than real Large
Language Model (LLM) inference, and (b) the trust graph is seeded
synthetically or from Stanford Network Analysis Project (SNAP)
datasets rather than growing organically. All other components-
Bayesian Lower Confidence Bound (LCB) scoring, Cumulative Sum
(CUSUM) detection, Personalized PageRank (PPR) computation,
cooldown tracking-run identically in simulation and production.
Our contributions:
(1) We show that EigenTrust-style trust propagation [1] fails
under coordinated attack at 30% adversary ratio, with the
failure replicating on two real-world Bitcoin trust graphs
and persisting at N=5,000 (Section 4).
(2) We identify an exploration-cost effect: Thompson sampling
is net-negative under adversarial load on sparse graphs
because adversaries occupy the high-uncertainty tail. We
isolate this via component ablation at 10 seeds per cell
(Section 4).
(3) We characterize the density boundary where this effect
reverses, confirmed on three independent dense topologies
at≥1.2% edge density (Section 5).
(4) We test a Behavioural adversary designed to exploit greedy
concentration. The exploration-cost finding survives this
falsification at n=10 (Section 4).
(5) We document the fraud-openness trade-off that greedy se-
lection introduces, including two failed recovery attempts
with mechanistic explanations (Section 7).
(6) We show that the exploration-cost finding is conditional
on routing visibility: under partitioned views with local
evidence, greedy selection produces 6.8× higher fraud than
Thompson sampling, establishing an exploration-visibility
trade-off (Section 7.3).
2 Related Work
Trust propagation systems.EigenTrust [ 1] applies power iteration
to local trust ratings to yield a global score per node. PeerTrust [5]
adds transaction context and rater credibility. Both implicitly treat
dense reciprocal endorsements as reliable. Our experiments point
the other way: a coordinated ring can seize the eigenvector because
power iteration settles on the densest cluster first. TRACE does not
rely on propagating trust. Instead, it uses PPR distance, entropy,
and clique density todetectsuspicious behaviour, causing densely
connected adversarial groups to accumulate penalties rather than
gain reputation score.
Sybil resistance.SybilGuard [ 6] and SybilLimit [ 7] depend on
bounded-length random walks to exploit sparse connections sep-
arating honest users from Sybil-controlled regions. The main as-
sumption behind these methods is that creating links into the honest
region is expensive. This works well in friendship graphs (bilat-
eral consent required) but not economic graphs: an adversary can
buy edges by completing a few cheap jobs, and the sparse-cut
guarantee falls apart. Douceur [4] formalized the underlying impos-
sibility: without a central authority, identity creation is unbounded.
TRACE’s detection signal is the edge-to-job ratio. Nodes with many
trust edges but few completed jobs are statistically anomalous, and
the anomaly persists even when attackerscancreate edges, because
edges without proportional work leave a distributional gap that
legitimate providers do not exhibit.
Adversarial bandits and exploration.TRACE’s routing inherits
the exploration-exploitation tension from the bandit literature (Rob-
bins and Monro [8] onward). UCB regret bounds come from Auer et
al. [9]; Thompson sampling bounds from Agrawal and Goyal [10];
the stochastic-versus-adversarial landscape is surveyed by Bubeck
and Cesa-Bianchi [11]. Lykouris et al. [12] are closest to our setting;
they study bandits under adversarial reward corruption and show
degradation proportional to the corruption budget. Our twist is dif-
ferent: the adversary chooses which arms are malicious (not which
rewards are corrupted), and 30% of the population is compromised.
The empirical result-Thompson sampling actively hurting-sits out-
side their theoretical coverage. Pulling an arm here means sending
money, not just accumulating regret in a bound.
Decentralized marketplaces and reputation.Bittensor [ 13] uses
trust-weighted miner consensus for inference routing. Filecoin
avoids trust altogether with cryptographic storage proofs. Dellaro-
cas [3] documents how anonymity and cheap switching create
manipulation incentives with no offline analogue. TRACE sits in
a different niche: work quality is subjective (syntactically valid
output can be semantically garbage), cryptographic verification
is unavailable, and the attacker wants money, not bandwidth or
consensus influence.

3 System and Threat Model
3.1 Marketplace Model
We represent the marketplace as a tuple M=(𝑂, 𝑃, 𝐽 , 𝑇) : buyers
𝑂, providers 𝑃, jobs 𝐽 priced in satoshis, and a trust graph 𝑇 de-
rived from observed economic interactions. Each job is associated
with a specific capability requirement, a price 𝑝 𝑗 , and a binary
outcome: success or default. The router can observe 𝑇 and the his-
tory of outcomes, but it cannot verify deriectly the true identity
of a provider. This extends Resnick et al. ’s [2] reputation model by
adding graph-structural signals and treating adversarial coordina-
tion as a first-class concern.
3.2 TRACE Architecture
TRACE scores each provider via four components:
(1) Bayesian LCB.Beta-Binomial posterior over job comple-
tion rate (prior Beta(1,3)). The 95% lower confidence bound
serves as the reliability estimate. Cold-start providers sit
near the prior mean until evidence accumulates.
(2) Default-risk penalty.Exponentially-smoothed default
probability, combined with CUSUM change-point detec-
tion [14] for identifying abrupt Behavioural shifts that sig-
nal a transition from cooperative to adversarial Behaviour
patterns.
(3) Graph signals.PPR [ 15] from an honest seed ( ≥30 jobs,
≤10% failure rate), weighted counterparty entropy, and
Sybil-risk scoring based on the edge-to-job ratio that detects
identity fabrication [4].
(4) Coordination detection.Clique penalty for dense trust-
edge neighborhoods; contamination penalty for connec-
tions to flagged providers. These signals detect the struc-
tural fingerprints of collusion rings without propagating
trust through the graph, distinguishing TRACE from Eigen-
Trust’s [1] propagation-based approach.
The utility score combines these as:
𝑢=𝛼·𝑠 trace−𝛽·𝑑risk−𝛾·𝑐norm+𝛿·𝑡net+𝜖·𝑚cap−𝜆·𝑝sybil−𝜇·𝑝clique (1)
with(𝛼, 𝛽, 𝛾, 𝛿, 𝜖, 𝜆, 𝜇)=(0.40,0.30,0.15,0.10,0.10,0.35,0.25).
Weight derivation.These weights were not grid-searched. They
reflect the relative contribution of each component as measured by
the ablation in Section 4: 𝛼= 0.40because the Bayesian LCB is the
largest single contributor to honest/malicious separation; 𝜆= 0.35
because disabling the Sybil penalty raises sybil-cluster fraud by
75%. A perturbation analysis (±20% on each weight independently,
180 cells) shows that no single-weight perturbation changes mean
fraud by more than 2.3 sats-well within the 1𝜎 band of the baseline
(4.3 sats on strategic-default). The results are not sensitive to the
specific weight vector.
3.3 The No-Bandit Variant
TRACE’s default selection uses Thompson sampling [16] over Beta
posteriors weighted by utility. Theno-banditvariant turns Thomp-
son sampling off and picks the highest-utility eligible provider every
time (greedy argmax). Everything else stays the same-same scoring,
same filters, same graph signals. It is a single boolean flag in the
codebase. The experiments below show that this one flag changes
fraud outcomes by 56–86%, which is why the comparison matters.
3.4 Threat Model
We evaluate four attack profiles at 30% malicious ratio with uniform-
random placement, spanning the spectrum from uncoordinated
individual defection to adaptive game-theoretic exploitation:
(1) Strategic-default.Solo adversaries calibrate default prob-
ability to maximise fraud below detection thresholds. No
coordination among attackers; each operates independently,
relying on statistical camouflage rather than graph manip-
ulation.
(2) Collusion-ring.Coordinated adversaries inflate mutual
trust scores during a build-up phase, then exploit inflated
scores to attract routing. This attack targets trust propaga-
tion systems [1] directly by manufacturing the dense edge
patterns that eigenvector methods interpret as trustworthi-
ness.
(3) Sybil-cluster.Adversaries create multiple identities con-
nected by trust edges, attempting to capture disproportion-
ate routing share through identity multiplication [4]. This
tests whether routing policies can resist the fundamental
vulnerability identified by Douceur in the absence of cen-
tralized identity verification.
(4) Game-Theoretic (GT).An adaptive adversary that mon-
itors its own score and defects only when it exceeds the
75th percentile of all active providers. The threshold is dy-
namic and self-calibrating, representing a patient attacker
that balances short-term fraud extraction against long-term
score maintenance.
4 Results: Sparse Topology
All experiments in this section use the same contract: N=1,000
agents, 30% malicious, 60 rounds × 5 jobs/round, 5 buyers, propaga-
tion delay of 2 rounds, no pre-existing trust edges (synthetic-empty).
Each cell was evaluated using ten different random seeds.

4.3 Behavioural Adversary Falsification
We designed an adversary specifically to test whether greedy con-
centration creates a vulnerability. The Behavioural GT adversary
observes its own routing share and defects only when that share
exceeds 1.5× fair-share-under-uniform AND the job price exceeds
1.2× median. It reads no internal scores. If greedy concentration

7 Discussion and Limitations
7.1 The Fraud-Openness Trade-off
Greedy selection reduces fraud but concentrates routing onto a
small set of proven providers (Figure 4). In our concentration probe
(strategic-default, n=3 paired seeds), TRACE-no-bandit served only
66 of 700 honest providers versus 125 for full TRACE-a 47% re-
duction in provider diversity (Table 8). This tension parallels the
proportional fairness problem in network resource allocation [21],
where optimizing for one objective (throughput, or in our case fraud
minimization) degrades another (fairness, or in our case provider
diversity), and improving the Pareto frontier requires additional
degrees of freedom.

7.2 Density Boundary
The exploration-cost advantage holds on sparse graphs and disap-
pears above ∼1.2% density, confirmed on three independent topolo-
gies (synthetic-dense, Bitcoin OTC [18], Bitcoin Alpha [18]). An
intermediate density sweep on collusion-ring (the most density-
sensitive attack) shows a sharp transition: fraud stays at 6.2 sats
through 0.8% density, then jumps 7.5 × to 47.1 sats at 1.2%. The
boundary lies between 0.8% and 1.2% edge density, indicating a
phase transition in the effectiveness of greedy filtering once pre-
existing edge density crosses a critical threshold.

Why does this happen? Premature lock-in. Sixty jobs is not
enough for Bayesian posteriors to converge. Greedy picks who-
ever succeeded first in that buyer’s local history-and with 30%
adversaries, that is a malicious provider roughly one time in three.
Thompson sampling prevents lock-in by occasionally trying al-
ternatives. With thin evidence, this is insurance, not waste. The
exploration cost from Section 4 is not intrinsic to the algorithm;
it depends on how much evidence the router has. Global visibility
(300 jobs) yields converged posteriors and exploration hitting the
adversary tail. Local visibility (60 jobs) yields uncertain posteriors
and exploration providing a safety net.
7.4 Robustness Checks
Adversary ratio.Sweeping 20%, 40%, 50% adversary ratios: strategic-
default fraud scales monotonically (2.4, 4.7, 4.6, 9.0 sats). No cliff-
edge failure. The filter does not claim to outperform the population
rate without oracle access.
Honest failure rate.Honest failure at 5%, 15%, 25%: no meaningful
fraud change (strategic-default: 5.6, 2.8, 4.2; GT: 31.0, 33.4, 34.8). The
trace-score filter discriminates on failurepattern, not rate-strategic
bursts cluster around high-value jobs; honest failures distribute
uniformly. Even at 25% honest noise, the two are separable.
Adversary threshold.The GT adversary’s percentile gate (p50–
p90) produced identical fraud (31.0 sats) across all thresholds under
no-bandit. The result is driven by the routing policy’s filtering, not
the adversary’s trigger calibration. Regardless of where the adver-
sary sets its defection threshold, TRACE’s coordination detection
and LCB scoring constrain the adversary’s routing share to a level
where the gate rarely fires, rendering the specific percentile choice
irrelevant.
Weight sensitivity.Perturbing each of the 7 utility weights by
±20% independently (180 cells, 3 seeds each) produced a maximum
fraud delta of +2.3 sats from baseline-within the 1𝜎 band on every
attack. This robustness arises from the complementary nature of
the scoring components: when one signal is weakened by a weight
reduction, the remaining signals compensate because they cap-
ture partially overlapping aspects of adversarial Behaviour from
different structural perspectives.
7.5 Limitations
Binary outcomes.Job outcomes are strictly binary. Real infer-
ence providers produce a quality spectrum-a response may be
syntactically correct but semantically wrong. Our Bayesian LCB
cannot differentiate catastrophic failures from marginal ones. Ex-
tending to graded outcomes requires a multinomial posterior and a
richer fraud metric that captures the severity distribution, which
in turn demands quality assessment capabilities beyond binary
success/failure classification.
Static adversary strategies.Each attack follows a fixed script. A
real adversary would switch strategies under detection pressure,
adapting its Behaviour based on observed score changes. Our Be-
havioural GT adversary approximates adaptation via routing-share
observation, but a reinforcement-learning adversary that optimizes
fraud extraction longitudinally remains untested. Such an adversary
could potentially discover strategy sequences that exploit TRACE’s
detection latency in ways that fixed scripts cannot.
Market concentration.The 47% drop in served honest providers
creates a barrier to entry. Newcomers cannot accumulate evidence
because they never get routed to, producing a self-reinforcing in-
cumbency advantage. Our two recovery attempts (loose and tight
round-robin) both failed, suggesting the tension between fraud-
defense and newcomer-discovery is fundamental to any system
that selects providers based on accumulated evidence [2].

7.6 Future Work
Visibility-conditioned exploration.Section 7.3 showed that explo-
ration’s value flips with evidence volume. One concrete mechanism
is to gate Thompson sampling on the utility gap between the top
two candidates. A large gap (relative to cohort variance) implies a
clear leader-skip exploration. A small gap implies posteriors have
not separated-explore as insurance. The gap statistic is already com-
puted in our scoring pipeline; we have not yet wired it into the
selection logic or tested whether it resolves both regimes simulta-
neously.
Community-aware Sybil detection.The edge-to-job ratio works
on synthetic graphs but fires false positives on real graphs where
honest users also cluster tightly. A fix is to run Louvain or Leiden
first, then measure each node’s clustering against its own commu-
nity baseline rather than a global constant. Honest tight-knit groups
stop triggering the alarm; adversarial clusters spanning multiple
communities still get flagged.
Longitudinal adversary adaptation.Our adversaries follow scripts.
An RL adversary that watches its own score trajectory and learns to
maximise fraud over hundreds of episodes would be a harder test-it
might discover strategies like burning an identity to reset detec-
tion state, or timing defections to coincide with high honest-failure
noise so CUSUM cannot distinguish the signal.
8 Conclusion
We evaluated four trust-routing policies under coordinated adver-
sarial pressure across 160 experimental cells at N=1,000 and a scale
run at N=5,000. The principal findings are:
• EigenTrust [1] routes 81.7% of jobs to Sybil identities across
all 10 seeds, with the pathology persisting at N=5,000 and
on two real-world Bitcoin trust graphs [18, 19].
• Disabling Thompson sampling (greedy selection) reduces
fraud by 56–86% across all four attack types on sparse
graphs, with the largest effect on collusion-ring: 6.4 vs.
46.5 sats.
• TRACE-no-bandit achieves the lowest fraud on 3 of 4 at-
tacks at both N=1,000 and N=5,000, with collusion-ring
fraud 14× lower than EigenTrust at scale (17.6 vs. 244.4
sats).
• A Behavioural adversary designed to exploit greedy concen-
tration fails to invert the finding: TRACE-no-bandit fraud
remains at 3.4 sats versus 27.8 for full TRACE.
• The greedy advantage vanishes above ∼1.2% edge density
via a sharp phase transition, confirmed on three indepen-
dent dense topologies.
• Under partitioned views (60 jobs per buyer), greedy selec-
tion produces 6.8× higher fraud than Thompson sampling,
establishing an exploration-visibility trade-off that bounds
the sparse-topology findings.
These results carry a clear implication: the optimal exploration
policy cannot be fixed. With global visibility and converged pos-
teriors, exploration routes payments to the adversary tail; with
local visibility and thin evidence, exploitation locks onto whichever
provider happened to succeed first. Bridging these regimes requires
conditioning exploration on evidence volume-a direction we leave
to future work under visibility-conditioned exploration.
